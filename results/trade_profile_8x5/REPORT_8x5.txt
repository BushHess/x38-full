## Two distinct strategy families

The 5 strategies split into 2 families with fundamentally different trade profiles:
  - E0-class (E0, E5, E0+EMA1D21): 172-207 trades, 5.8-6.9 day avg hold, exposure ~100%
  - Low-exposure class (SM, LATCH): 65 trades, 14.4 day avg hold, exposure ~10-19%

This difference pervades every metric. Direct numeric comparison across families
requires normalizing by exposure or trade count; within-family comparison is straightforward.


## T1 — Win rate is uniform; edge comes from W/L ratio

All 5 strategies win 40-44% of trades — the canonical trend-following profile.
Alpha is encoded in the W/L ratio (avg win / avg loss):
  - SM/LATCH: 3.64-3.84  (higher because holds capture larger moves)
  - E0-class: 3.09-3.30
  - E0+EMA1D21 best in E0-class: WR 43.6%, PF 1.72

Implication: no strategy has an entry timing advantage. The difference is
how long they hold and how they exit.


## T2 — EMA1D21 regime filter cuts max loss streak

Max consecutive losses:
  - E0=9, E5=10, SM/LATCH=8, E0+EMA1D21=7

E0+EMA1D21 achieves the shortest max loss streak across all 5 strategies.
This is direct evidence that the D1 EMA(21) regime filter removes entries
during adverse conditions. The practical consequence: lower drawdown anxiety
during live trading.


## T3 — Holding time confirms turnover differences

SM/LATCH hold 2.3x longer than E0-class (14.4 vs 6.4 days).
P90 holding time: SM/LATCH=29 days vs E0=13.7 days.
E0+EMA1D21 holds slightly longer than E0 (6.9 vs 6.4 days) because the
regime filter blocks some short-duration entries.


## T4 — MFE/MAE: edge ratio is universal; magnitude scales with hold time

Key finding: median edge ratio is 1.31-1.88 for ALL strategies — the typical
trade sees favorable excursion only ~50% larger than adverse excursion.
This is a slim edge per trade; profitability comes from asymmetric sizing
of the tail.

SM/LATCH have 2x the MFE (16% vs 9%) but also 1.5x the MAE (5.6% vs 3.6%).
The additional MFE is mechanically driven by longer holding periods, not
better entry timing.

Negative MFE-MAE correlation (rho = -0.22 to -0.30) across all strategies:
trades that reach high favorable excursion tend to have low adverse excursion.
This is the signature of a valid trend-following system — good trades run
clean, bad trades whipsaw.


## T5 — Trail stop is the profit engine; trend exit is a cost center

For E0/E5/E0+EMA1D21, two exit types exist:
  - trail_stop: 84-92% of trades, positive avg return (3.0-3.1%), win ~46-48%
  - trend_exit: 8-16% of trades, negative avg return (-1.2 to -1.7%), win 14-23%

Trend exits act as a loss-cutting mechanism — they exit quickly (1.4-1.8 days)
when the trend reverses. The cost is ~$27k-61k total PnL drag across the
backtest, but this is insurance against larger drawdowns.

E0+EMA1D21 has the fewest trend_exit trades (14/172 = 8.1% vs E0 31/192 = 16.1%).
The regime filter pre-screens entries, reducing the need for the trend_exit
safety valve by half. This is a structural improvement.

SM/LATCH have only one exit mechanism (floor_exit), which simplifies their
trade profile but eliminates the early-exit option.


## T6 — Payoff concentration: all strategies are top-trade dependent

Top-10 trades account for 122-143% of total PnL — meaning the remaining
trades collectively have negative PnL. This is mathematically inherent to
trend-following: many small losses, rare large wins.

Gini coefficients (0.54-0.61) confirm high inequality in trade outcomes.

E5 has the lowest concentration (HHI=0.0154, effective_N=64.7), meaning
its PnL is most evenly distributed. SM/LATCH have the highest concentration
(effective_N=24-25) — a smaller pool of trades makes each one more impactful.


## T7 — JACKKNIFE: THE CRITICAL ROBUSTNESS TEST

This is the most important new result. Removing the top-K best trades and
recomputing Sharpe reveals how fragile each strategy is:

  Drop top-5 → Sharpe change:
    E5:              0.894  (−27.3%)   ← MOST ROBUST
    E0+EMA1D21:      0.827  (−29.6%)
    E0:              0.775  (−31.9%)
    SM:              0.138  (−83.2%)   ← FRAGILE
    LATCH:           0.147  (−82.2%)   ← FRAGILE

  Drop top-10 → Sharpe:
    E5:              0.637            ← still meaningful
    E0+EMA1D21:      0.535
    E0:              0.462
    SM:             −0.512            ← negative = unprofitable without top trades
    LATCH:          −0.506

Interpretation:
  - E0-class strategies survive the removal of their top-10 trades with
    Sharpe 0.46-0.64 — alpha exists broadly, not just in outliers.
  - SM/LATCH go negative when top-10 trades are removed — their entire
    edge is concentrated in ~10 out of 65 trades (15% of trades carry
    the entire strategy).
  - E5 is the most jackknife-robust: even after removing the 10 best
    trades, Sharpe remains 0.637.

Bottom-removal (dropping worst trades) shows symmetric sensitivity:
  - SM/LATCH improve most dramatically (+28-30% Sharpe from dropping 10 worst)
  - Confirms high concentration on both tails.

This analysis provides mathematical proof that SM/LATCH are fragile strategies
despite their attractive headline Sharpe. The REJECT verdict from Study #41
is reinforced: SM/LATCH depend on a handful of trades for all their alpha.


## T8 — All strategies are fat-tailed and right-skewed

Jarque-Bera p = 0.0000 for all 5 — trade returns are highly non-normal.
All show positive skew (2.5-3.7) — right tail is heavier than left.
This is desirable for trend-following: rare large wins, bounded small losses.

Excess kurtosis ranges from 7 (SM/LATCH) to 19 (E0). Higher kurtosis in
E0-class reflects more extreme outlier trades relative to the bulk.
SM/LATCH have lower kurtosis because their fewer trades sample the tail
distribution less densely.

P1-P99 range: SM/LATCH span -16% to +71% vs E0 -9% to +46%. The wider
range for SM/LATCH reflects their longer hold times and correspondingly
larger per-trade excursions.

## Key conclusions:

1. E5 and E0+EMA1D21 are the most robust strategies by trade profile.
   E5 wins on jackknife stability; E0+EMA1D21 wins on max loss streak (7 vs 10).

2. SM/LATCH have attractive per-trade metrics (PF 2.6-2.7, E[r] 4.7-4.8%)
   but are structurally fragile — their edge is concentrated in ~10/65 trades.
   This confirms the REJECT verdict from Study #41.

3. All 5 strategies share the same fundamental profile: low win rate (~42%),
   positive skew, fat tails, and top-trade dependency. The difference is
   degree of concentration, not kind.

4. No strategy escapes the trend-following dependency on rare large moves.
   The jackknife test quantifies this dependency, and E5/E0+EMA1D21 manage
   it best by having more trades (207/172 vs 65) over which to distribute risk.
